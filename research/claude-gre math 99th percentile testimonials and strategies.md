# How Top Scorers Prepared for the GRE Mathematics Subject Test — And How to Encode It in an Anki-Based Study App

## TL;DR
- The single most consistent strategy across high-scorer accounts is **calculus-and-linear-algebra-first, speed-over-depth**: ETS's own specification is Calculus 50%, Algebra (elementary/linear/abstract/number theory) 25%, Additional Topics 25%, so the calculus-plus-algebra block is ~75% of the test; the low-yield tail (topology, complex analysis, numerical analysis) is highly stereotyped and should be triaged with pattern-memorization, not deep study — one self-reported 970 scorer wrote "Most of your score will be correlated to your ability to solve calculus and linear algebra problems fast."
- Top scorers treated the exam as a **speed/recognition test, not a proofs test**: they conserved the ~6 released ETS forms (GR9768, GR0568, GR1268/GR1768, GR8767, GR9367, and the newest GR3768), took them timed, built error logs, and memorized recurring question types and time-saving formula shortcuts.
- These accounts are a **biased, self-selected sample** (mostly strong math majors who already knew most content), so their strategies correlate with success but are not proven to cause it — the app should encode the *process* (calculus-weighted sequencing, interleaved timed drills, error logs, conserved mock forms) while resisting the survivorship-driven overconfidence of "2 weeks is enough."

## Key Findings
1. **Calculus + linear algebra is the high-yield core.** ETS's fact sheet states "Approximately 50 percent of the questions involve calculus and its applications" and "About 25 percent of the questions in the test are in elementary algebra, linear algebra, abstract algebra, and number theory." Every credible scorer concentrates here. A 99th-percentile scorer: "I mostly studied calculus, linear algebra and ODE. This was a majority of the test."
2. **The "additional topics" tail is low-yield but easy points if pattern-matched.** Complex analysis is "By far the most stereotyped section. Only two types of questions." Graph theory/logic/algorithms are "about one question about each. Super basic stuff."
3. **Princeton Review's "Cracking the GRE Mathematics Subject Test" (Steven Leduc, 4th Edition, ISBN 9780375429729) is the near-universal core book** — the publisher lists "1 full-length practice test... Over 150 guided practice problems... Over 200 drill questions" — supplemented by Stewart's Calculus for problem volume and Schaum's Outlines for targeted drilling. REA is widely panned for its tests but valued by some for extra practice volume.
4. **Released ETS forms are scarce and precious.** Strong advice: don't burn them all early; take them timed; review every miss.
5. **Timing is the dominant failure mode.** The recurring failure story is running out of time and/or careless errors, not lack of knowledge.
6. **Timelines range from ~2 weeks (already-strong majors) to 3–4 months / a full summer** (rust or weaker background). The shorter timelines come from people who already had the content.

### How they allocated time: high-yield core vs. low-yield tail
Top scorers explicitly de-prioritized the low-yield tail. The weighting is not just forum lore — it is ETS's own published specification: Calculus 50%, Algebra (elementary, linear, abstract, number theory) 25%, Additional Topics 25%. The additional-topics questions are stereotyped enough to be handled by pattern recognition. One scorer noted the contemporary forum belief that calculus's effective weight feels even higher than 50% ("is it true that about 90% of the test is on calculus" — a question posed to a 99th-percentile scorer). Several accounts warn against over-investing in numerical analysis and esoteric stats — the tutor guide notes the REA book wastes "30 pages worth of Lagrange interpolation, Bernstein polynomials, and splines," none of which appeared on real tests. — https://www.mathsub.com/resources/

### How they used practice tests and reviewed mistakes
The dominant method: take released forms timed, then mine errors for recurring patterns and drill those specifically (Schaum's, Wikipedia, textbook sections). The UChicago guide prescribes an explicit alternating cycle: non-official practice → released test (timed) → learn missed topics → repeat, conserving the newest form for last. Many scorers redo the same problems repeatedly to find faster solution paths ("I redo the problems again and again, trying to find shortcuts" — https://mathematicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=3302).

### Speed and timing tactics (66 questions / 170 minutes)
ETS confirms "approximately 66 multiple-choice questions... Testing time is 2 hours and 50 minutes; there are no separately-timed sections," which mathsub frames as "this gives you an average of about 2½ minutes per question." Tactics top scorers used:
- Memorize generalized answers to recurring question formats to bank time for harder items.
- Skip and flag time-sinks immediately; return later.
- Guess on everything at the end (no penalty since 2017).
- Use process of elimination / back-substitution across the 5 choices.
- Build calculation speed by grinding calculus textbook exercises (Stewart).

### Common failure modes overcome
- **Running out of time:** "i didnt have NEARLY enough time. i answered only about 2/3rds of the questions." — https://mathematicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=207
- **Calculus rust / forgotten content:** a returning major "breezed through the first 40 or so questions ... but really faltered in the last third," finishing ~74th–75th percentile. — https://www.letsrun.com/forum/flat_read.php?thread=4362250
- **Careless errors:** JonMLTR "missed 3 easy questions that I could have answered easily." — https://mathematicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1065
- **Over-studying low-yield topics / wrong books:** wasted time on REA's numerical-analysis depth.
- **Wasting practice tests without structure:** an MIT student "wasted a bunch of time over the summer doing practice tests" the first time, then improved from 51st to 79th percentile with better method. — https://math.mit.edu/gummi/GRE.html

### Feature-mapping: encoding these strategies into the Anki-based app

1. **Calculus-first, topic-weighted study plan (prerequisite DAG).** Default plan sequences single-variable calculus → multivariable calculus (R3, computational) → linear algebra → ODEs first, with abstract algebra and the additional-topics tail layered later. Weight card volume and review frequency by ETS's published exam representation (50% calc, 25% algebra, 25% tail). Directly encodes Ranks 1–2. The prereq DAG enforces "learn calculus before ODE systems / multivariable before vector calculus."

2. **Stereotyped-pattern decks for the low-yield tail.** Pre-built Anki decks of the recurring question archetypes: complex analysis = {Cauchy-Riemann, residue/contour}; logic/graph/algorithms = one-archetype-each; numerical analysis = "order these numbers." Encodes Rank 2 and "memorize generalized answers."

3. **Interleaved, timed mixed-topic problem sets at ~2.5 min/question.** A "mock-pace" mode that mixes topics and enforces the ETS-implied ~2½-min/item budget, training pacing and topic-switching. Encodes Rank 7 and the speed emphasis. Interleaving mirrors the real test's randomized topic order.

4. **Error-log / spaced-review feature.** Every missed practice question auto-generates a tagged card (by topic + sub-concept), routed into spaced repetition with extra frequency — operationalizing boredmathguy's "identify every single question you've gotten wrong." Encodes Rank 5.

5. **Conserved-mock readiness scoring.** Track which of the ~6 released ETS forms have been "spent," warn against using them all early, and reserve the newest (GR3768/GR1768) for a final readiness check. Calibrated readiness score maps raw → scaled/percentile using a conversion chart, weighted by calculus/linear-algebra accuracy specifically. As a concrete anchor, mathsub's 2019 chart states "getting 66 correct on GR1768 indicates you are likely to get at least a scaled score of 910 (96th percentile)" — useful as a built-in conversion reference, with a caveat that such charts drift over time. Encodes Ranks 4 and 8.

6. **Formula/shortcut "cheat-sheet" card type.** A dedicated card category for time-saving identities (∫ln x dx = x ln x − x, secant integrals, common Taylor series, Cauchy-Riemann, integrating factor). Encodes Rank 6.

7. **Coverage map + triage dashboard.** A visual map of topic coverage vs. ETS exam weight, flagging over-investment in low-yield topics (numerical analysis, esoteric stats) and under-coverage of high-yield calc/linear algebra. Encodes the triage philosophy and guards against the REA-style failure mode.

8. **Pacing/guessing trainer.** Drills the "skip-and-flag," "guess-everything-at-end (no penalty since 2017)," and "check the 5 choices / back-substitute" tactics.

## Recommendations
**Stage 1 (build the default plan now):** Ship a calculus-first, exam-weighted study plan backed by a prerequisite DAG (single-var → multivar → linear algebra → ODE, then abstract algebra, then tail). Pre-load Princeton Review-aligned topic decks (4th edition's ~150 guided + ~200 drill questions are a natural card source) plus a "stereotyped pattern" deck for the tail. This encodes the single highest-consensus strategy.

**Stage 2 (differentiate with process features):** Add the auto-error-log → spaced-repetition loop, the timed interleaved mixed-set "mock-pace" mode at ~2.5 min/question, and a formula/shortcut card type. These convert the most-corroborated *process* habits (timed practice, error mining, shortcut memorization) into defaults.

**Stage 3 (readiness & guardrails):** Add conserved-mock tracking across the ~6 released forms, a calibrated readiness score weighted toward calculus/linear-algebra accuracy, and a coverage-map dashboard that actively warns against over-studying low-yield topics.

**Benchmarks that change the plan:**
- If a user's calculus + linear algebra timed accuracy is already high (≈≥80%), shift effort to the tail and pure speed (the "2 weeks is enough" profile) — but only then.
- If practice-form percentile plateaus around the 80th percentile, flag diminishing returns (per boredmathguy) and surface the "is a retake worth it vs. other application components" decision.
- If timed completion rate is the bottleneck (running out of time), down-weight new content and up-weight pacing drills and shortcut cards.

## Caveats
**Self-selection and survivorship bias (pervasive).** The people who write detailed prep accounts online are overwhelmingly those who *already had* strong backgrounds (math majors, REU participants, people with high general-GRE Quant) or who succeeded and want to share. Their strategies correlate with high scores but may not cause them; "I studied 2 weeks and got 970" almost always hides years of prior coursework. Treat every confident timeline as a lower bound for an already-strong student, not a recipe for the median user.

**Tiny sample sizes vs. corroboration.** The calculus-first, speed-focused, conserve-mocks, error-log advice is corroborated across many independent accounts and expert guides — treat as robust. Single-anecdote claims ("2 weeks is enough," exact topic counts on a specific test) should be treated as illustrative, not reliable.

**Dated material and delivery change.** Key guides (UChicago ~2018, Katie Waddle 2019, mathsub conversion data 2019) predate the newest released form (GR3768) and the post-2001 difficulty increase noted by tutors. Crucially, the subject test moved to **computer-delivered administration in September 2023** (per ETS), so older first-person accounts describing paper bubble-sheets and booklet annotation are now historical — the "mark and review" feature is now an on-screen tool. Percentile/scaled-score conversions also drift over time.

**Scoring-era confusion.** Scoring changed in 2017: there is now no wrong-answer penalty (answer everything). Sources referencing a 1/4-point deduction reflect pre-2017 directions (GR1268 vs. the re-issued GR1768). The app must make the current rule explicit.

**The score's actual value.** Multiple credible voices (MIT, faculty cited on the forum) note GRE-subject performance is weakly correlated with PhD success and that returns diminish sharply past the 80th percentile — relevant for how hard to push a user toward 990. Many programs have also dropped the subject-test requirement entirely.
